# Water Conservation Guidelines for Educational Campuses

## 1. Smart Metering & Leak Detection
* Implement sub-metering at key points (hostels, academic blocks, mess kitchens, laboratories) to monitor consumption and detect abnormal flows indicating leaks.
* Regularly check float valves in overhead tanks. A faulty float valve can waste up to 2,000 liters of water daily due to overflow.
* Check taps and pipes for slow leaks: a tap dripping at one drip per second wastes over 11,000 liters of water per year.

## 2. Low-Flow Aerators & Sensor-Based Faucets
* Install aerators on all public and restroom taps. Aerators mix air into the water stream, reducing flow from 15 liters per minute to less than 6 liters per minute without compromising water pressure.
* Install dual-flush toilet mechanisms. Dual flush systems use 3 liters for half-flush (liquid waste) and 6 liters for full-flush (solid waste), compared to traditional single-flush systems that consume 9 to 12 liters per flush.
* Implement push-button or motion-sensor faucets in high-traffic student restrooms to eliminate water waste from taps left running.

## 3. Landscape & Irrigation Management
* Utilize drip irrigation systems for gardens and flower beds instead of overhead sprinklers. Drip systems apply water directly to the root zone, reducing evaporative loss by up to 60%.
* Irrigate landscape areas during early morning or late evening hours to minimize evaporation losses from wind and heat.
* Plant native, drought-resistant trees, shrubs, and groundcovers (xeriscaping) that naturally thrive in local climates with minimal supplemental watering.

## 4. Rainwater Harvesting (RWH)
* Roof runoff collection: Connect building roofs to filtering chambers and redirect clean rainwater into storage sumps or groundwater recharge wells.
* Calculate harvesting potential: 1 mm of rainfall on 1 square meter of roof yields approximately 0.9 liters of harvestable water (adjusting for runoff coefficient).
* Pervious pavements: Ensure pathways and parking areas use permeable interlocking concrete pavers to let rainwater infiltrate the ground, replenishing the local water table rather than creating surface runoff.
